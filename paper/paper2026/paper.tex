\section{Introduction}

   Giant molecular clouds (GMC) are among the dominant reservoirs of cold material in galaxies(\textbf{cite}) and represent a crucial phase of the galactic interstellar environment.
   The interstellar medium consists of diffuse and dense gas, interstellar dust(\textbf{cite}), and is threaded by magnetic fields(\textbf{cite}) while being continuously influenced by energetic particles(\textbf{cite}).
   Together, these components regulate how galaxies evolve by controlling when and where stars can form(\textbf{cite}).

   Within giant molecular clouds, physical conditions are far from uniform. Instead, these objects exhibit strong spatial and temporal variations in gas density(\textbf{cite}), temperature(\textbf{cite}), velocity structure(\textbf{cite}), and magnetic field properties(\textbf{cite}).
   Turbulence(\textbf{cite}), gravity(\textbf{cite}), magnetic forces(\textbf{cite}), and radiative processes interact nonlinearly(\textbf{cite}), producing a complex internal structure ranging from diffuse envelopes to compact, gravitationally bound cores(\textbf{cite}).
   These environments enable a wide variety of processes, including the collapse of dense regions into young stellar objects(\textbf{cite}), which subsequently influence the evolution of the GMC through feedback mechanisms such as outflows(\textbf{cite}), as well as the formation of chemically complex species.(\textbf{cite})
   Understanding the internal makeup of molecular clouds is therefore essential for linking small-scale star formation physics to large-scale galactic evolution. 


   Accurate measurements of gas density in molecular clouds form the foundation for many key quantities in star formation studies.
   Density estimates are used to infer the mass of the cloud substructures, which in turn define the dense core mass function (DCMF).
   The DCMF is of particular interest because of its close connection to the stellar initial mass function (IMF), providing an important observational link between cloud-scale physics and the final masses of stars.
   Uncertainties in density measurements therefore propagate through this entire chain, affecting our understanding of how molecular clouds translate their mass into stars.

   Despite its importance, determining the density and mass distribution of molecular clouds remains a challenging task.(\textbf{cite})
   Observationally, these quantities are often inferred from dust continuum emission, which requires assumptions about dust temperature(\textbf{cite}), opacity(\textbf{cite}), and gas-to-dust ratio (\textbf{cite}).
   The problem is intrinsically complex and ill-posed, as different combinations of physical parameters can produce similar observational signatures (e.g two dense cores can be in the same line of sight).
   As a result, inverting dust emission data to recover quantities that need an understanding of the three-dimensional structure is highly non-trivial and subject to significant degeneracies.


   In recent years, machine learning has emerged as a powerful tool for addressing such complex inference problems in astrophysics.
   A wide range of applications now exist across the field, from source classification\textbf{cite} to parameter estimation\textbf{cite} and forward modeling\textbf{cite}.
   At the same time, modern numerical simulations are able to capture many of the relevant physical processes governing molecular clouds, including turbulence, gravity, magnetic fields, and radiative effects.
   These simulations provide realistic, physically motivated datasets that are well suited for training and testing machine learning models on problems that are otherwise difficult to tackle observationally.


   However, the rapidly growing diversity of machine learning architectures makes it non-trivial to identify which approaches are best suited for specific physical questions.
   Different network designs may capture different aspects of the underlying physics, yet their capabilities and limitations in the context of molecular cloud studies are not always well understood.
   Developing physically meaningful metrics to evaluate and compare these models can provide valuable insight into what each architecture is actually learning.
   Such an understanding can help move beyond methods that rely solely on dust emission and open the door to more sophisticated applications that combine multiple observables and probe increasingly complex physical regimes.



\section{Methods}

   In this work, because our goal is to study dense structures in molecular clouds, such as prestellar cores, 
   we choose to estimate, using neural networks, the mass-weighted average volume density along the line of sight:
   \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:mw_density}
      \nmh(x,y)=\frac{\int_{0}^{L}n_H(x,y,z)m(x,y,z)dz}{\int_{0}^{L}m(x,y,z)dz},
   \end{equation}
   from the column density map derived from dust emission:
   \begin{equation}
      \label{eq:column_density}
      \Nh(x,y)=\int_{0}^{L}n_H(x,y,z)dz,
   \end{equation}
   where $x$ and $y$ are the plane-of-sky positions, $z$ is the position in line of sight axis, $L$ the cloud length, $\nh$ the hydrogen volume density, $m(x,y,z)=\nh(x,y,z)\mu m_\text{H}$.

   Thus, our benchmark focuses on a $\mathrm{2D}\rightarrow\mathrm{2D}$ learning problem.
   Note also that the mass-weighted average density can be converted into the average density of dense cores using a small correction (see Appendix~\ref{appendix:mw_correction}).

   Except for making the simulations, all steps, from creating the datasets and
   training neural networks to applying them to observations, are performed using our code \texttt{POLARIScore}\footnote{\url{https://github.com/ZackRibeiro/POLARIScore}}
   ,which includes our implementations of network architectures using \texttt{PyTorch} \citep{paszkePyTorchImperativeStyle2019}. More parameters for the trained neural networks can be found in Appendix~\ref{appendix:char_networks}.

\subsection{MHD Simulations and Datasets}

   Neural networks need to be trained on synthetic data, where they can learn the relevant correlations and underlying physics.
   For segmentation tasks, such training data can consist of human-generated or algorithm-generated labels on observations (see, for example, filament-identification studies \citep{alinaMaLeFiSentaMachineLearning2022}).
   \\ However, in our task of estimating a physical quantity in molecular clouds, we do not have access to true observational values. This is where simulations become essential, since they provide full access to all required physical quantities.
   
   The properties of the simulations used to train the neural network are therefore extremely important and directly influence the network's performance.
   \textbf{talk about properties of the cloud Orion B}

   These datasets are created using magnetohydrodynamical (MHD) simulations of a star-forming molecular cloud: 
   \textit{ORION} \citep{ntormousiCoreStellarMass2019}, publicly available through the GALACTICA\footnote{\url{http://www.galactica-simulations.eu/db/STAR_FORM/ORION/}} database and shown in Figure \ref{fig:sim_coldens}. 
   \\These simulations are not isothermals with cooling on and $\gamma=5/3$, model MHD turbulence and have a size of 66.1 pc with an initial resolution of 0.01 pc, reaching a few hundred AU using
   an AMR grid. The simulations were performed using RAMSES \citep{teyssierCosmologicalHydrodynamicsAdaptive2002}, with self-gravity applied, star-formation
   treated using Lagrangian sink particles and ideal MHD resolved and no chemistry.

   \\ Then, images with a resolution of $128\times128$ pixels and a physical size of $3.33$pc are randomly cropped from the three faces.
   An image is retained in the dataset if it meets several conditions:
   \begin{itemize}
      \item It is spatially distant enough from an already explored region, meaning it does not overlap with an area covered by an existing image.
      \item It does not contain a disproportionately large fraction of very low-density regions, i.e, the edges of the simulation (WNM).
      \item It exhibits significant density variations within the explored regions. A score is based on the smoothness of the image using Laplacian of the density, favoring regions with filaments over those with nearly homogeneous density. (See Appendix~\ref{appendix:score}) 
   \end{itemize}

\subsection{Training}

   Training a neural network is a crucial step in achieving good performance.
   There are multiple choices that we need to make to obtain optimal results.
   \begin{itemize}
      \item We want the model, with N free parameters (weights), to
      converge to a global minimum of the loss function rather
      than getting stuck in a local well. Therefore, the choice
      of optimizer, which performs backward error propagation
      (adjusting the model's weights), is important. Here, we
      use ADAM \citep{kingmaAdamMethodStochastic2017}.
      \item Loss function: A well-chosen loss function is essential for
      accurately representing the problem. Here, we generally
      use the mean squared error or minimizing the negative log-likelihood depending on the architecture.
      \item Learning rate: The learning rate is another critical
      factor, it determines the step size the optimizer takes
      in the loss function during each update (epoch). Ideally, it should decrease when the loss reaches a plateau. To
      manage this, we use a \textit{scheduler}.
   \end{itemize}

   Each epoch, i.e., learning step, the model processes all the dataset, column density images, and predicts their mass-weighted density.
   A way to artificially increase the size of the training batch is to apply random transformations to the images at each epoch
   \citep{yangImageDataAugmentation2023}, such as random rotations and random vertical or horizontal flips (no deformations). 
   Afterward, the prediction loss is computed using the chosen loss method, and backward error propagation is performed. Then, a new epoch begins, repeating the same steps.

\subsection{Machine learning}
   In this benchmark, we explore three different neural network architectures.
   We start with a robust and widely used model for image-to-image regression tasks, the UNet \citep{ronnebergerUNetConvolutionalNetworks2015,oktayAttentionUNetLearning2018}.
   We then test more recent state-of-the-art conditioned generative approaches, namely Conditional Invertible Neural Networks (cINNs) \citep{ardizzoneConditionalInvertibleNeural2021} and Diffusion Models \citep{hoDenoisingDiffusionProbabilistic2020}.   
   
   We do not include Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) \citep{goodfellowGenerativeAdversarialNetworks2014} in this work mainly because they are known to suffer from mode collapse \citep{kossaleModeCollapseGenerative2022}.   
   \subsubsection{Convolutional Neural Network}
      Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) were introduced (\cite{fukushimaNeocognitronSelforganizingNeural1980}, \cite{lecunBackpropagationAppliedHandwritten1989})
      as a specialized architecture for grid-like data such as images or maps.
      Instead of connectiong every input to every neuron (as in multi-layer perceptrons), CNNs restrict connections to local receptive fields.
      Each neuron in a convolutional layer is connected only to a small region of the input, and the same set of learnable weights (a filter or kernel) is shared across different spatial locations.
      This enables CNNs to learn spatially invariant features such  as edges, textures and structure morphologies.
      
      Mathematically, for a 2D input $X$ (e.g a column density region), the convolution operation with a filter $W$ is given by:
      \begin{equation}
         \label{eq:convolution}
         Y_{i,j}=f(\sum_{m=1}^{M}\sum_{n=1}^{N}W_{m,n}\cdot X_{i+m,j+n}+b),
      \end{equation} 
      where $W$ is the learnable convolutional kernel, $X$ is the input feature map, $Y$ is the output feature map, $f$ is a nonlinear activation function such as $\text{ReLU}$, $b$ is the learnable bias term.
      
      In this work, we are using an Attention-U-Net (\cite{ronnebergerUNetConvolutionalNetworks2015},\cite{oktayAttentionUNetLearning2018}) architecture, shown in Figure~\ref{fig:unet}.
      It features a symmetric encoder-decoder structure where the encoder captures high-level features of the column density map through convolution (Eq~\ref{eq:convolution}) and max pooling operations, while the decoder gradually reconstructs the mass-weighted density map using upsampling layers.
      \\A key feature of U-Net is its \textit{skip connections}, which allow direct information flow between corresponding encoder and decoder layers, preserving spatial details lost during downsampling.

   \subsubsection{Conditional Invertible Neural Network}
      \label{sec:cinn}

      However, state-of-art networks used today are not deterministic architectures such as UNet, but stochastic models that sample from probability distributions.
      
      The goal of these networks is to sample from the conditional data distribution $p_\mathrm{data}(X|c)$ where $X$ is the target random variable (here $\nmh$) and $c$ the conditioning information (from observable, here $\Nh$).
      If the inverse problem is ill-posed, i.e if one cannot uniquely infer the mass-weighted average volume density map $\nmh(x,y)$ from the 2D column-density map $\Nh(x,y)$, the generative network instead learns to sample physically plausible solutions drawn from the full posterior distribution 
      \begin{equation}
         p(\nmh(x,y)|\Nh(x,y)).
      \end{equation}

      To make sampling tractable, we map the unknown complex distribution to a simple latent one, typically a standard multivariate Gaussian: 
      \begin{equation}
         p_z(Z)=N(0,I),
      \end{equation}
      where $Z$ represents the latent variables.

      The network then learns a bijective transformation $f$ between $X$ and $Z$, conditioned on $c$:
      \begin{equation}
         Z=f(X,c),~X=f^{-1}(Z,c)
      \end{equation}
      The approach used in conditional invertible neural networks (cINN,~\cite{ardizzoneConditionalInvertibleNeural2021}) is to train \textit{the forward direction} 
      \begin{equation}
         f: X \rightarrow Z
      \end{equation}
      such that the pushforward of the data distribution becomes Gaussian:
      \begin{equation}
         f_*(p_\text{data}(X|c))=p_z(Z).
      \end{equation}

      This is achieved through normalizing flows \citep{rezendeVariationalInferenceNormalizing2016}, which transform a complex distribution into a simple one via a sequence of invertible transformations: $f_1,...,f_K$:
      \begin{equation}
         Z=f_K\cdot f_{K-1}\cdot...\cdot f_1(X,c).
      \end{equation}
      For each invertible transformation $f_k$, the change-of-variable formula gives the exact likelihood:
      \begin{equation}
         p_\text{data}(X|c)=p_z(f(X,c))|\det\frac{\partial f(X,c)}{\partial X}|.
      \end{equation}

      During training, the cINN maximizes the log-likelihood of the data under this model.
      At inference time, we draw $Z\sim N(0,I)$ and obtain samples of the physical solution via the inverse mapping: $X=f^{-1}(Z|c)$.
      
      To ensure reasonable computation time, each transformation $f_k$ in the flow must be easily invertible, and its Jacobian determinant must be efficient to compute.
      cINNs achieve this by using conditional coupling blocks (CCBs).
      Let the input of a coupling block be split (in features channels) as $u\rightleftarrows (u_1,u_2)$, and the output be $v\rightleftarrows (v_1,v_2)$.
      A typical forward transformation of a CCB is:
      \begin{equation}
         \label{eq:cinn_ccbs_forward}
         \left\{
         \begin{aligned}
            v_1 &= u_1\odot\exp (s_1(u_2,c))+t_1(u_2,c), \\
            v_2 &= u_2\odot\exp (s_2(v_1,c))+t_2(v_1,c),
         \end{aligned}
         \right.
      \end{equation}
      where $s_i$, $t_i$ are scale and translation functions implemented by sub-networks (e.g MLPs). 
      These sub-networks do not need to be invertible because they are only evaluated in the forward pass; the coupling block itself remains analytically invertible.
      The inverse transformation is obtained by algebraic rearrangement of Equation~\ref{eq:cinn_ccbs_forward}:
      \begin{equation}
         \label{eq:cinn_ccbs_reverse}
         \left\{
         \begin{aligned}
            u_2 &= (v_2-t_2(v_1))\odot\exp (-s_2(v_1)) \\
            u_1 &= (v_1-t_1(u_2))\odot\exp (-s_1(u_2))
         \end{aligned}
         \right.
      \end{equation}

      Because each coupling block is triangular in structure, its Jacobian is also triangular.
      Thus the log-determinant of the Jacobian is simply the sum of the scaling terms:
      \begin{equation}
         \log |\det J|=\sum s_1(u_2,c)+\sum s_2(v_1,c),
      \end{equation}
      where the sums run over features and spatial dimensions.
      This makes CCBs computationally efficient while retaining exact invertibility and tractable likelihood.

   \subsubsection{Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Model}
      Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs, \cite{hoDenoisingDiffusionProbabilistic2020}) generate samples by gradually transforming white noise, i.e a sample from the latent Gaussian prior $p_z=N(0,I)$, into a data sample $x_0\sim p_\text{data}$.
      This is achieved through a sequence of learned denoising steps parameterized by a neural network, typically a UNet.

      The forward diffusion process progressively destroys structure by adding Gaussian noise with a predefined variance schedule $\beta_t$. In the continous-time limit, this process is described by the stochastic differential equation (Itô SDE):
      \begin{equation}
         \label{eq:ddpm_sde}
         dx=f(x,t)dt+g(t)dW_t=-\frac{1}{2}\beta(t) x_t dt+\sqrt{\beta(t)} dW_t,      
      \end{equation} 
      where $W_t$ is a Wiener process. 
      Here:
      \begin{itemize}
         \item $g(t)=\sqrt{\beta(t)}$ is the diffusion strength,
         \item $f(x,t)=-\frac{1}{2}\beta(t)x$ is the drift term,
         \item $t\in[0,T]$ parametrized the diffusion trajectory. 
      \end{itemize}
      This SDE is a variant of the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process, whose solutions converge to a Gaussian distribution \citep{uhlenbeckTheoryBrownianMotion1930}.
      The process is variance-preserving, sice as $t\rightarrow\infty$, $x_t\sim N(0,I)$.

      To generate data, DDPMs simulate the reverse-time SDE:
      \begin{equation}
         \label{eq:ddpm_sde_reverse}
         dx= [f(x,t)-g(t)^2\nabla_{x_t}\log p_t(x_t)]dt+g(t)d\overline{W_t},
      \end{equation}
      where $\overline{W_t}$ is a Wiener process running backward in time.
      The quantity
      \begin{equation}
         \nabla_{x_t}\log p_t(x_t)
      \end{equation}
      is the score function, i.e the gradient of the log-density of the noisy data at time $t$.
      Because the exact score is intractable, the network learns an approximation $s_\theta(x_t,t)\approx\nabla_{x_t}\log p_t(x_t)$.

      In DDPM parameterization, the neural network instead predicts the noise
      \begin{equation}
         s_\theta(x_t,t)\approx\frac{\epsilon_\theta(x_t,t)}{\sigma_t},
      \end{equation}
      where $\sigma_t=\sqrt{1-\overline{\alpha_t}}$ is the standard deviation of the forward noising kernel.
      
      The forward noising process has the closed-form solution:
      \begin{equation}
         x_t = \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}, x_0 + \sqrt{1 - \bar{\alpha}_t}, \epsilon,
         \qquad \epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,I),
      \end{equation}
      where $\overline{\alpha_t}=\prod_{i=1}^{t}(1-\beta_i)$ encodes the cumulative noise schedule.

      Thus, training a DDPM consists of learning to predict the injected noise $\epsilon$, which in turn provides the score needed to solve the reverse SDE and sample realistic data from pure Gaussian noise.
      
      In the conditional setting, the observable $\Nh$ is incorporated by concatenating it with the noisy sample $x_t$ at the input of the UNet, ensuring that each denoising step is conditioned on the available physical information.

\subsection{Application}
   The trained neural networks are applied to Orion~B, a giant molecular cloud located at a distance of approximately $400\,\mathrm{pc}$ \citep{mentenDistanceOrionNebula2007}. 
   Herschel observations \citep{konyvesPropertiesDenseCore2020,andreFilamentaryCloudsPrestellar2010} reveal a fragmented filamentary structure, with star formation primarily concentrated in dense clumps.

   The networks are applied to the column density map derived from dust emission. Prestellar cores are identified using multiwavelength source extraction and spectral energy distribution (SED) fitting of the dust emission, and are taken from the catalogue of \citet{konyvesPropertiesDenseCore2020}.

   Because the neural networks are trained on regions with a physical size of $3.3\,\mathrm{pc}$, the column density map is downsampled by a factor of 4.3 to match the spatial resolution of the training dataset. The observation is then divided into overlapping patches of $128 \times 128$ pixels, each of which is provided independently as input to the neural network.

   The patches are allowed to overlap in order to provide multiple contextual environments for each position in the map and to reduce edge effects inherent to convolutional architectures. The final prediction at each pixel is obtained by averaging the outputs from all overlapping patches covering that pixel.

   In addition of the neural networks, we also apply a parametric fit of the form
   \begin{equation}
      \label{eq:gaussian_fit}
      f(x) = \sum_{i=1}^{4} a_i \exp\left( -\frac{(x - \mu_i)^2}{2\sigma_i^2} \right),
   \end{equation}
   derived from simulations, to model the conditional distribution $\nmh(\Nh)$ (see Fig.~\ref{fig:sim_correlation}).

   During the training of all neural networks, we use the mean squared error (MSE) computed in logarithmic space to monitor the generalization performance of the models. 
   Specifically, the MSE loss is evaluated on the validation set and is defined as
   \begin{equation}
      \label{eq:nn_mse}
      \mathrm{MSE} = \mathbb{E}\left[ \left( \log Y_{\mathrm{pred}} - \log Y_{\mathrm{sim}} \right)^2 \right],
   \end{equation}
   where $Y_{\mathrm{pred}}$ and $Y_{\mathrm{sim}}$ denote the predicted and simulated (true) mass-weighted average volume-density maps, respectively.

   If one considers only the MSE values reported in Table~\ref{tab:nn_metrics}, UNet ($0.19$) and DDPM ($0.25$) appear to be the most performant models, ahead of the cINN ($0.50$). 
   However, the MSE is an average over all regions and environments and therefore does not fully capture the ability of a network to adapt to different physical conditions. 
   In particular, because of its averaging nature, a model may achieve a low MSE by performing well in diffuse background regions while still exhibiting significant errors in filamentary structures, whose spatial filling factor is much smaller than that of the cold neutral medium (CNM).

   \end{equation}

\section{From mass-weighted averages to core densities}
   \label{appendix:mw_correction}

   %Exprimer sous Nd et Ld, ecrire ce que vaut nd, changer Nh par 10^22 dans corrections, plot rapport inverse.

   In this work, the neural networks aim to predict the mass-weighted average volume density:
   \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:mw_density_appendix}
      \nmh(x,y)=\frac{\int_{0}^{L}n_H(x,y,z)m(x,y,z)dz}{\int_{0}^{L}m(x,y,z)dz},
   \end{equation}
   where $x$ and $y$ are the plane-of-sky positions, $z$ is the position in line of sight axis, $L$ the cloud length, $\nh$ the hydrogen volume density, $m(x,y,z)=\nh(x,y,z)\mu m_H$. 

   If we discretize the density along the l.o.s into $M$ components with constant volume density in it
    (e.g for a datacube simulation, $M$ corresponds to the number of cells), then Eq \ref{eq:mw_density_appendix} becomes 
   \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:mw_density_disc}
     \nmh =\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{M}n^2_{\text{H},i}\times L_i\mu m_\text{H}}{\sum_{i=0}^{M}n_{\text{H},i}\times L_i\mu m_\text{H}}=
     \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{M}n^2_{\text{H},i}L_i}{\sum_{i=0}^{M}n_{\text{H},i}L_i},
   \end{equation}
   where $L_i$ is the length of component $i$.
   This quantity is chosen mainly because this weighted average density is close to the average density of dense structures along the l.o.s such as dense cores.
   \\ 
   However, when a dense core is embedded by a very diffuse region (i.e outside filmentary structures), a correction is required to convert the mass-weighted average density
   $\nmh$ into the true average volume density of the dense core $n_{\text{H},c}$. To simplify notation, we denote the average core density simply by $n_c$ and volume densities without the $_\text{H}$.
   \\
   Consider two components along the line of sight ($M=2$):
   \begin{itemize}
    \item A diffuse medium with length $L_d$ and with constant density $n_d$.
    \item The dense core with length $L_c=2r_c$ where $r_c$ is the core radius, and with constant density $n_c$. 
   \end{itemize}
   Equation \ref{eq:mw_density_disc} then reduces to
   \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:mw_density_twomeds}
     \nmh=
     \frac{n^2_{c}L_c+n^2_{d}L_d}{n_{c}L_c+n_{d}L_d},
   \end{equation}
   To estimate $n_c$, two parameters must be assumed: $n_d$ and $L_d$, while $L_c$ is derived from observations as two times the core radius.
   The column density: $\nh=n_{c}L_c+n_{d}L_d$ removes one degree of freedom, giving 
   \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:mw_density_twomeds_ld}
     n_c=
     \Nh\left(\frac{1+\sqrt{1-(\frac{L_d}{L_c}+1)(1-\frac{\nmh L_d}{\Nh})}}{(L_c+L_d)}\right)
     ,\text{ if }L_d\text{ assumed.}
   \end{equation}
   \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:mw_density_twomeds_nd}
     \text{or }n_c=
     \frac{n_d}{2}\left(1+\sqrt{1-\frac{4\Nh}{L_c n_d}(1-\frac{\nmh}{n_d})}\right)
     ,\text{ if }n_d\text{ assumed.}
   \end{equation}
   But these corrections can lead to $\nmh$ being higher than $n_c$, because the conditions $n_d>0$ or $L_d>0$ are not enforced. 
   To rectify this, we clamp the core density as $n_c = \max(n_c, \nmh)$, with $n_c$ computed from one of the previous formulas. 

   If one wants to assume specific values for $L_d$ and $n_d$:
   \begin{equation}
    \label{eq:mw_density_twomeds_ndld}
     n_c=
     \frac{\nmh}{2}\left(1+\sqrt{1+\frac{4L_d n_d}{L_c \nmh}(1-\frac{n_d}{\nmh})}\right)
   \end{equation}

   \end{equation}

\section{Score-based training set}
   \label{appendix:score}
   Creating a training dataset is a crucial step in building an effective model. In the particular case of molecular clouds structures,
   we want to avoid overtraining on the borders of the simulation (WNM), as these regions contain less valuable information for our objective compared to the dense regions with filaments and cores.
   To achieve this, we implement an additional filtering step:
   \begin{itemize}
      \item We compute a "\textit{score}" $s(\mathrm{image})$ for each new candidate area beffore adding to the dataset. 
      This score is calculated using a custom function that aligns with our training objectives.
      \item We then apply a filter function $f(s)$. If a randomly generated number $r$ in the range $[0,1]$ satisfies $r<f(s)$, the region is accepted and added to the training set.
      I.e $f(s)$ is the probability to accept and add an area with a score of $s$ in the dataset.
   \end{itemize}
   This approach ensures that the dataset focuses more on important structures (filaments and cores) rather than sparse or less informative regions.
   
   We can also use multiple scoring functions, each capturing different aspects of the dataset, and combine them using weighted ($w_i$) sums to account for various criteria.
   Mathematically, the final score $s$ can be expressed as:
   \begin{equation}
      s=\sum_{i}s_iw_I
   \end{equation}
   
   This score can be made for the column density image $s(\Nh)$ and the volume density image $s(\nmh)$.
   \subsection{Score functions}
   \paragraph{Smoothness score}
   The score function used when generating our training set needs to be based on the smoothness of the selected area.
   This is because if an area is entirely within a diffuse region, it will exhibit low spatial frequency variation (i.e, only large-scale structures)
   and lack filaments.
   \\ To ensure that each selected image contains both diffuse regions and some filaments, we use the variance of the smoothness as a criterion.
   The smoothness itself is defined as the Laplacian of the image, which highlights areas with sharp density changes (such as filaments).
   \begin{equation}
      \label{eq:score_smoothness}
      s_\Delta(x)=\mathrm{Var}[\Delta(\mathrm{log}(1+x)-\mathrm{min}[\mathrm{log}(1+x)])],
   \end{equation}
   where x is the pixel value. The variance in equation~\ref{eq:score_smoothness} is to get a mix of dense and high-density regions.
   \paragraph{Difference score}
   A basic criterion that can be chosen is the difference between the normalized column density and the normalized average volume density (Eq~\ref{eq:score_difference}).
   This helps identify regions where the local density distribution differs significantly between the two representations.
   \\For example, when using mass-weighted average density, this criterion will highlight dense regions, since areas with high volume density but relatively lower column density indicate localized dense structures along the l.o.s.
   \begin{equation}
      \label{eq:score_difference}
      s_{\mathrm{ref}}=\mathrm{Var}[\frac{\Nh-\mathrm{min}(\Nh)}{\mathrm{max}(\Nh)-\mathrm{min}(\Nh)}-\frac{\nmh-\mathrm{min}(\nmh)}{\mathrm{max}(\nmh)-\mathrm{min}(\nmh)}]
   \end{equation}

   \subsection{Filter functions}
   As a filter function $f(s)$, we use a sigmoid:
   \begin{equation}
      f(s)=\frac{1}{1+\mathrm{exp}(-A(s-B))},
   \end{equation}
   where $A$ is the step length and $B$ the offset, and are chosen empirically.

\section{Characteristics of the Trained Networks}
\label{appendix:char_networks}

In this appendix, we summarize the training settings and important details for all networks, to facilitate reproducibility. 
All networks were trained on an NVIDIA GeForce RTX 2070 SUPER with 8~GB of VRAM.

   \subsection{UNet}
      Architecure: \cite{oktayAttentionUNetLearning2018}
      
      UNet is a straightforward model to train, consistently converging to the loss minima.
      \begin{itemize}
         \item Number of layers: 5
         \item Number of base features (scaling with depth: $\text{base\_features} \times 2^i$): 64
         \item Convolutional block used: DoubleConvBlock (two convolution layers with ReLU per block)
         \item Attention: Gated Attention Block applied at each layer \citep{oktayAttentionUNetLearning2018}
         \item Learning rate: $1\times10^{-4}$ at initialization, using the ADAM optimizer
         \item Data normalization: logarithmic scale (base 10)
      \end{itemize}
   \subsection{cINN} 
      Architecture: \cite{ardizzoneConditionalInvertibleNeural2021}
      
      When choosing the cINN parameters, a trade-off must be made between the number of layers in the encoder and the number of base features. 
      This is because the MLP subnetworks in the coupling blocks concatenate the conditions provided by the encoder with the input data $u$ or $v$ (see Section~\ref{sec:cinn}). 
      Consequently, the encoder should remain relatively shallow so as not to overload the data flow within the coupling blocks.
      \begin{itemize}
         \item Encoder depth (number of layers): 3
         \item Encoder base features: 32
         \item Coupling blocks per layer: 3
         \item Subnetwork architecture (for predicting $s$ and $t$): Conv $\rightarrow$ ReLU $\rightarrow$ Conv $\rightarrow$ ReLU $\rightarrow$ Norm $\rightarrow$ Conv $\rightarrow$ Clamp
         \item Downsampling before coupling blocks: Haar wavelets
         \item Attention: none. We did not obtain stable training when using multi-head attention. Although attention mechanisms can, in principle, improve performance by enabling the model to capture long-range contextual dependencies, they introduced significant training instabilities in our experiments.
         \item Learning rate: $1\times10^{-3}$ at initialization, using the ADAM optimizer
         \item Data normalization: scaled to the range $[-1,\,1]$, which is important for generative models that require sampling:
            \begin{equation}
               \label{eq:normalization}
               \left\{
               \begin{aligned}
                  \overline{\Nh} &= \frac{\log_{10}(\Nh)-19}{6}\times2-1 \\
                  \overline{\nmh} &= \frac{\log_{10}(\nmh)}{8}\times2-1
               \end{aligned}
               \right.
            \end{equation}
      \end{itemize}
   \subsection{DDPM}
      \begin{itemize}
         \item UNet depth (number of layers): 3
         \item UNet base features: 32
         \item Prediction target: $v$-parameterization (rather than $\epsilon$), following \citet{karrasElucidatingDesignSpace2022}
         \item Noise schedule: cosine $\beta$-schedule, with $\beta$ ranging from $10^{-4}$ to $0.02$ over 500 diffusion timesteps
         \item Inference: the full 500 diffusion steps are not used at inference; instead, sampling is performed with 20 steps using a reduced-noise schedule
         \item Attention: multi-head self-attention (8 heads) applied at the bottleneck and final resolution levels \citep{vaswaniAttentionAllYou2023}
         \item Learning rate: $1\times10^{-4}$ at initialization, using the ADAM optimizer
         \item Data normalization: same as for the cINN (see Eq.~\ref{eq:normalization})   
      \end{itemize}

\section{Demande d'heure}

La fonction de masse initiale des étoiles (IMF) est une composante essentielle de la théorie de la formation stellaire,
mais l'origine de sa masse caractéristique demeure mal comprise. De nombreux travaux suggèrent qu'elle pourrait émerger des propriétés de la turbulence supersonique dans les nuages moléculaires.
En parallèle, la fonction de masse des cœurs denses (CMF), précurseurs directs des étoiles, reste mal contrainte aux faibles masses en raison de limitations observationnelles, la détection reposant majoritairement sur l'émission thermique des poussières.
L'émission moléculaire, bien que plus complexe à exploiter, permet cependant de lever des dégénérescences le long de la ligne de visée et d'améliorer significativement l'estimation des masses.

Dans ce contexte, l'objectif est de développer des méthodes de deep learning (UNet, cINN, DDPM...) pour la détection et la caractérisation des cœurs denses à partir de cartes de poussières et de cubes hyperspectraux de CO.
L'entraînement de ces modèles nécessite des simulations numériques réalistes et à haute résolution. Un ensemble de simulations de turbulence MHD isotherme auto-gravitante sera donc réalisé avec le code IDEFIX, couvrant un espace de paramètres représentatif des nuages moléculaires (nombres de Mach sonique et Alfvénique). 
Les simulations consisteront en des boîtes périodiques turbulentes avec auto-gravitée (setup de Mathew and Federrath, 2021)  de 5 à 10 pc de largeur, avec une résolution minimale de 1024³ afin de résoudre correctement la longueur de Jeans et les cœurs denses.

Neuf simulations sont prévues, correspondant à trois valeurs du nombre de Mach sonique et trois valeurs du nombre de Mach Alfvénique, chacune évoluée sur cinq temps de retournement de la turbulence (environ 5e5 à 1e6 pas de temps). Sur la base des performances mesurées du code IDEFIX (environ 3e8 cellules/s sur GPU Mi300),
le coût de calcul est estimé à environ 800 heures GPU par simulation, soit un total d'environ 7000 heures Mi300.
Le pipeline d'analyse, d'entraînement des réseaux de neurones et d'application aux observations est déjà opérationnel (Z.Ribeiro and P.Hily-Blant, en préparation) et des simulations pilotes à plus faible résolution ont permis de valider la faisabilité numérique et la pertinence physique d'utiliser des boxes turbulentes périodiques.
